# Electric Bicycles Guide

## Motors

Electric bicycles use one of two motor types: hub motors (mounted in the wheel) or mid-drive motors (mounted at the bottom bracket). Mid-drive motors leverage the bike's gears, giving better climbing performance and efficiency, especially on hills.

Power is measured in watts. In Europe, pedal-assist bikes (EPACs) are limited to 250 watts continuous output and 25 km/h assisted speed. In the United States, Class 1 and Class 2 bikes top out at 20 mph, while Class 3 bikes reach 28 mph.

## Batteries

The battery is the heaviest and most expensive component of an electric bicycle. Capacity is measured in watt-hours (Wh). A typical commuter battery holds between 400 and 700 Wh.

Range depends on terrain, rider weight, assist level, and wind. A 500 Wh battery delivers roughly 40 to 80 km of range in normal conditions. Lithium-ion batteries lose capacity over time and should be stored at around 50 percent charge when not used for long periods.

## Pedal Assist Levels

Most e-bikes offer three to five assist levels. Eco mode extends range, while Turbo or Boost mode prioritizes power. Some bikes also include a walk-assist mode that pushes the bike at walking speed when you are off the saddle.

A torque sensor measures how hard you press the pedals and scales motor output accordingly. A cadence sensor only measures whether you are pedaling, which gives a less natural ride.
